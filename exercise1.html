Questions 46–53
Ronan
I work in a 46 traditional office environment doing a typical nine-to-five job. I like my job, but it's annoying that my commute to work takes an hour and a half each way and most of my work could really be 47 online from home. But my boss doesn't seem to trust 48 we will get any work done if left to our own devices, and everyone in the company has to clock in and out every day. It's frustrating that they feel the need to monitor what we do so 49 instead of judging us based on our task performance, like most companies do these days.

Jo
I used to do a typical five-day week, but I wanted to spend more time with my children before they start school, so I talked to my boss to have a hybrid schedule. After negotiating with him, we decided to cut my working week 50 to a three-day work week. I've noticed, though, that my workload 51 decreased in the slightest! 52, I find myself having to take work home just so that I can meet the deadlines. It's wearing me out trying to juggle work with 53 my family.

✅ Answer Explanations

46
a) fairly — "Fairly" means "quite" or "moderately" and is the right degree adverb here. "Extremely" would be too strong for a typical office, and "constantly" is an adverb of frequency that doesn't make sense before "traditional".
47
a) done — The structure is "could be done online", a passive infinitive. "Started" and "explained" don't fit grammatically or in meaning here.
48
c) that — After "trust", we need a conjunction to introduce a noun clause: "trust that we will get work done." "Where" and "which" don't work in this grammatical context.
49
b) closely — "Monitor closely" is a fixed collocation meaning to watch carefully. "Nearly" means almost (quantity), and "promptly" means quickly — neither fits with "monitor".
50
c) down — "Cut down" is a phrasal verb meaning to reduce. "Cut in" means to interrupt, and "cut up" means to chop — neither fits the context of reducing working days.
51
b) hasn't — The present perfect is needed because this is a situation that started in the past and continues now. "Didn't" (simple past) and "hadn't" (past perfect) don't match the present-result meaning.
52
a) On the contrary — This connector introduces the opposite of what was just said: workload hasn't decreased; in fact, the opposite is true. "Even though" and "Despite" are concession markers, not contrast connectors at the start of a new sentence.
53
b) looking after — "Looking after" means taking care of (family). "Taking up" means starting a hobby, and "bringing along" means accompanying someone — neither fits the idea of caring for family.
